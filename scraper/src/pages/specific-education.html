381-319-DW
Perspectives in Anthropology
1 section
  • Visual Anthropology
  • 00001
  • Katarzyna Wolfson
  • The topic for this course will be visual anthropology. The main interests of visual anthropology are: 1) production of visual documentation of people, practices, and cultures in an ethnographic context, and 2) analysis of visual representations of people, practices, and cultures. How we see things and how what we see is understood will be examined using anthropological concepts, theories and methods. This course is concerned with the study of visual systems and material culture. An analysis of visual and material processes such as photography, film and new media will allow students to consider how visual representation is constructed, interpreted and contested.

381-401-DW
Advanced Studies in Anthropology
1 section
  • Material Culture
  • 00001
  • Maciej Domanski
  • The topic is the anthropology of material culture – a broad range of material phenomena that emerge through human cultural activity. Examples include artifacts, buildings, landscapes or environments. The extent to which humans interact with these “objects” is one of the features that set them apart from other species. Besides their purely utilitarian functions, “objects” are also meaningful and play an active role in various areas of social life such as economic exchanges, the exercise of power, the construction of identities, institutions, and relations. The complexities of these processes will be considered over the semester. We will focus on what material culture is, what it does and what it means to us. We will explore the major conceptual and analytical frameworks in which the research in material culture is situated. Using a hands-on approach, students will also plan and complete a project that involves an analysis and display of a selected aspect of material culture.

Why food? In short, decisions about what to eat and why permeate our everyday experience. While it is a topic usually considered profoundly individual, this course begins with the premise that the subject of food is deeply sociological. Moreover, as we will discover throughout the course, it serves as a nexus by which we can explore the relationship between what we consider quite personal decisions to broad and societal issues. Students will have the opportunity to carry out individual studies on a broad range of topics, from the relationship between food and health to the effect of culture and what you eat to the effects of food insecurity on children. Students will also have the opportunity to participate in an ongoing research project in Montreal directed at studying our local food system.
