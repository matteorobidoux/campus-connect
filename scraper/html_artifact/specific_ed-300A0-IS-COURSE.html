  • Entrepreneurship
  • 00003
  • Menouar Boulahfa
  • The entrepreneurial process involves the identification and evaluation of an opportunity; the decision whether or not to exploit it; the efforts to obtain the resources; the process for organizing those resources in some cases into an innovative combination and the development of a strategy for the new venture. Students will find out how innovative entrepreneurs created their businesses and will discuss their profitable undertakings. Students will write about one chosen innovative entrepreneur through the lenses of two self-chosen social disciplines to integrate, on an individual basis, the learning achieved during the DEC program.

  • 00004
  • Carlos Velasquez Buritica, Elliot Kerr
  • Students in the Social Science Program take this course in their final term after having passed Research Methods (300-300-DW) and Quantitative Methods (360-300-DW). The Integrative Seminar provides the student with an opportunity to consider how the various social sciences can work together to contribute to understanding social phenomena. Using at least three (3) social science disciplines, students will explore a topic related to the seminar theme in a series of distinct stages, ultimately producing a long essay on this exploration. This integrative project will address some of the linkages and differences among social sciences and show how their combined use makes for a deeper understanding of the selected topic.

  • The X-Files: The Truth about Belief
  • 00022
  • Richard Soare
  • Questions concerning the origin and possible uniqueness of our place in the universe as sentient and reflective beings date back to the earliest instances of humankind’s introspection. Through the lens of history, philosophy, psychology, religion, etc. this course will explore conventional and unconventional hypotheses about these questions, evaluate their plausibility and attempt to delineate the similarities and differences between “belief,” “truth” and “knowledge”. Part of this process will be the opportunity for students to step into and out of the conceptual “box” in order to dissect commonplace assumptions about who we are, as a species, and whereto we are heading.

  • Controversial Issues
  • 00023
  • Melanie Doyle
  • The general theme for this IS class is “Controversial Issues” and this can apply to so many things: poverty, abuse, addiction, global warming, porn-culture, gender, social media, cryptocurrencies – and even happiness: the list goes on. Over the course of the semester, you will complete a research paper, drawing on peer-reviewed research from at least three social science disciplines, which takes a position on a debate within the field. The theme of this course is deliberately broad to allow students to focus on an issue that interests them; I leave it to each student to define “Issue” and apply the theme to whatever most intrigues them. The principal aim of the course is for you to develop a well-reasoned and well-researched argument on your topic

  • Vaccine Hesitancy and Belief in Conspiracy Theories
  • 00026
  • Robin Joseph Gagnon
  • This IS section will be devoted to an exploration of the causes of human irrationality and belief in unsubstantiated phenomena. Possible avenues of exploration will be examining the contribution of cognitive biases, propaganda, and persuasion, as well as the effects of personality types, normal psychological characteristics, and mental disorders. Students will use a multidisciplinary approach drawing from at least three different social science disciplines.

  • Ethical Issues in Canada
  • 00027
  • Calogero Miceli
  • This course deals with the social science phenomenon of the processes and procedures in the sphere of Psychology. The areas of research may include issues in clinical and social psychology. The students will select three disciplines related to their subject of research and will use appropriate methodological approaches from these disciplines to define the process of integration. As part of the methodology students will evaluate academic sources and databases, including those available on the Internet. Students will present their research findings in an oral presentation to the class. Next, by integrating feedback from teacher based on their written and oral work, students will produce a final paper to complete the integrative project by writing a paper of 4000 words.

  • Education
  • 00030
  • Cornelia Howell
  • In this IS, students will get a chance to search for answers to some of the many questions society has about education. How should children be educated? What are the goals of education? How has education changed over time? What should the job of educator entail? Where should funding for educational resources come from? And these questions merely scratch the surface. Students will get the chance to explore the topic cross-culturally, sociologically, psychologically, historically, and/or economically. Ultimately, we will take this opportunity to explore many facets of education.

  • Contemporary Threats to Liberal Democracy
  • 00033
  • Donal Gill
  • This course seeks to investigate the stability of governing institutions across liberal democracies in the 21st century. Contemporary challenges facing liberal democracies around the world today will be analyzed through a multidisciplinary social scientific perspective. The course will engage social science disciplines such as political science, sociology, history, psychology, economics etc. to assist in the writing of a research paper that investigates a core issue related to democratic values, governance and consolidation. Students will write a research paper on liberal democracy and democratic consolidation/deconsolidation before pursuing independent research on a related topic of their choosing.

  • Global Justice Issues?
  • 00034
  • Catherine Braithwaite
  • In today’s 21st century, issues of social justice, equity, and human rights for all have become an overriding concern for countries and communities around the globe. Issues as diverse and wide-ranging as racial equity, hunger and food security, gender equality, decolonization, and access to healthcare have increasingly driven government policy and citizen activism. Sometimes together, sometimes in opposition, nations and their populations have worked to effect change in the face of diverse global challenges. In this course, students will undertake an integrative multidisciplinary research project examining issues of global (in)justice in countries ranging from Canada to Brazil, Somalia, New Zealand, and the United States, and the steps we take in our attempts to create a just world.

  • Youth and Neighborhood
  • 00037
  • Nancy Rebelo
  • Neighbourhood is where home, school, services, and even friendships converge. As such, our most basic experiences and identities are often linked to the neighbourhood in which we grow up. In this course, students will explore how experiences of neighbourhood impact child development through three different social science disciplines. Students may choose to investigate themes such as social housing, food access, community support (health, social, recreational), urban vs. suburban, causes and consequences of neighbourhood-based social inequalities, etc. They may also choose a particular neighbourhood, in Montreal or elsewhere, to investigate its strengths and weaknesses related to child development. Students interested in pursuing careers in social services, education, or urban studies will have a chance to take a deeper look at these key issues related to how to create neighbourhoods that promote health, safety, inclusion, and happiness for children and families.

  • Social Justice Movements
  • 00039
  • Mark Beauchamp
  • The world as we know it exists in large part because of the forced movement of people across space. In this course, we will think about how different bodies have been moved by larger structures of power, at different points in our history. We’ll learn about the transatlantic slave trade, the displacement of Indigenous peoples, and the historical and continued exploitation of immigrant labour. An exploration of these histories will help us to develop our understandings of race, labour, land and power. From this foundation, we’ll embark on multidisciplinary investigations of contemporary issues, asking: how are today’s struggles for social justice rooted in the legacies of these histories?
