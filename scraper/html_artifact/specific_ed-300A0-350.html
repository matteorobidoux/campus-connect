Developmental Psychology
4 sections
  • 00001
  • Jacob Nuselovici
  • This course outlines the development of the child and how he/she is influenced by heredity, environment, family, school and social experiences. Emphasis is placed on the changes that occur in both physical and psychological attitudes during childhood. Discussions also focus on basic concepts and theories related to child development. In addition, developmental processes in adolescence, adulthood, and old age are considered.

350-401-DW
Advanced Studies in Psychology
4 sections
  • Thinking and Memory
  • 00001
  • Rajesh Malik
  • This course will investigate the workings of the human mind by considering the topics of perception, memory, thinking, judgment, and decision making. It will examine how these processes operate normally, as well as establishing when and how they are affected by biases we hold. Some of the specific issues the course will look at include: problems of human memory and its implications for the law and justice system, how people make day-to-day decisions about their finances and shopping needs, and suggestions for becoming better thinkers and informed decision makers.

  • Fundamentals of Learning
  • 00002
  • Stephane Gaskin
  • This course introduces students to the theories of learning in humans and non-human animals. Advanced concepts in classical conditioning, instrumental conditioning and memory will be discussed at much greater depth than in a general psychology course. Students will also learn about the stimulus control of behaviour, self-control, types of memory, and observational learning. In addition, students will learn to apply the learning principles covered in the course to their everyday lives and learn about the behavioural methods used in research and therapy. In line with the elements of competency, they will acquire knowledge of the field of learning through 1) devising the outlines of a behavioural journal in which 2) they will be asked to keep track of their behaviours in the various contexts of their daily lives and 3) identifying them with the concepts learned in class.
