Canadian History
3 sections
  • Crime and Injustices in Canada
  • 00001
  • Catherine Braithwaite
  • This course will survey the history of crime and criminal justice in Canadian society, from its English, French and First Nations’ origins to the present day.Topics may include French, English and Aboriginal systems of justice; the early criminal judicial system of New France; its transformation after the British conquest; prisons and penal reform; the question of social (in)justice and its relation to women, juveniles, and ethnic minorities; policing and the RCMP; the role of the state and national security, and any other topics of relevance to the world of crime and justice. These topics will be examined using concepts such as gender, crime, law, race, class, and human rights, while using a variety of historical sources and methodologies.

  • Crime and Society
  • 00002
  • Rachèle Caux
  • This course will survey the history of crime and criminal justice in Canadian society, from the English and French origins to the present day. The topics to be covered include the French and English systems of justice as they emerged from the Middle Ages (ordeals, inquisitorial procedure, witch craze…); the criminal judicial system of New France; the transformation after the British conquest; the judicial reforms of the Enlightenment and the introduction of the penitentiary; the Canadian justice system and its relation to women, juveniles and ethnic minorities; policing and the RCMP; capital punishment; and the present-day Canadian justice system. These topics will be examined using concepts such as gender, crime, law, race, class, and human rights, while using a variety of historical sources and methodologies.

20th Century History
5 sections
  • War and Conflict
  • 00001
  • Michael Wasser
  • This course will survey the history of the world, especially Europe and Asia, from c.1900 to c.2000. Subjects to be analyzed include the two World Wars, the Russian Revolution, the rise of fascism, the movement towards national liberation in Europe’s colonies and spheres of influence (especially South Asia), conflicts between non-Western nations (especially the Iran-Iraq War), and the Cold War. Concepts to be analyzed are demography, industrialization, race, gender, class, imperialism, socialism, fascism, nationalism, and religion. These events and concepts will be examined using various historical sources. Different historical methodologies will also be introduced.

  • 00002
  • Mark Theriault
  • This course will focus on the causes and consequences of World War I and World War II.   • Snapshots of the 20th Century
  • 00003
  • Deborah Shulman
  • Snapshots always capture a moment in time. However, they might also reveal far more. The 20th century was a remarkable period of exhilarating and tumultuous change and challenge. We will consider moments in time as an entrée to exploring various aspects of this period in order to understand its continuities and discontinuities. The Model T Ford (Michigan, Manchester, 1908-27) and Tiananmen Square’s ‘Tank Man’ (Beijing, 1989) are examples of those moments in time that represented something far more. In this course, we will traverse and consider key topics in 20th century history, and students will analyze issues using historical approaches and methodologies.

330-221-DW
Third World History
1 section
  • Colonization and Decolonization
  • 00001
  • Diane Shea
  • This course is a survey of European colonization and decolonization around the globe from the 1500s to the present. Starting in the Americas, the course follows colonization through Africa, Asia and the Middles East. The course examines the impact of colonization, the nationalist struggles and revolutions accompanying decolonization, and the ongoing problems of the post-colonial world. Students will analyze concepts such as imperialism, colonialism, decolonization, neo-colonialism, nationalism, revolution, racism, enslavement, genocide, ethnicity, and gender. Possible case studies include: Indigenous struggles in the Americas, Congo, Apartheid South Africa, Iraq, Afghanistan, China or Vietnam. Students will understand the forces, events and ongoing systemic structure of colonization. A variety of sources and methodologies are introduced.

Applied U.S. History
1 section
  • A Nation Fractured: Social Conflicts in 20th Century USA
  • 00001
  • Pierre L'Heureux
  • This course examines key social and cultural changes in the modern history of the United States, such as the struggle to reconstruct the Southern States following the Civil War, mass immigration and the nativist response to it, the culture war of the 1920s, the long civil rights movement, the revolutions of the 1960s and 1970s, and the rise of contemporary conservatism in the late 20th century. Various primary and secondary sources, such as cultural artifacts, historical accounts, movies, and academic analyses, will be used by students with the concepts of class, generation, gender, race, ethnicity, religion, nationalism, and regionalism to examine this theme. Students will also approach social and cultural changes in the modern history of the United States by applying several methodologies, including discourse and film analyses, comparative history, iconographic analysis, and statistics.

Applied 20th Century History
1 section
  • Art and Power in the 20th Century
  • 00001
  • Jocelyn Parr
  • This course falls into the category of Intellectual and Cultural History, which means it emphasizes changes in how society has thought and produced art. We begin in the early decades of the 20th century, tracing the way art (broadly defined) has worked both against and in collaboration with powerful political regimes and how it has intersected with times of war. In the second half of the century, these themes will intersect more closely with protest movements and changing mores around race, sex, gender and class. Students can expect to think about Fine Arts like painting, as well as documentary film, poetry, protest and pop music. We will look closely at Cold War Culture, Decolonization, Indigenous Sovereignty movements, and the rise of neoliberalism in the 80s.

Applied Social and Economic History
3 sections
  • The Family
  • 00001
  • Nancy Rebelo
  • This course will explore different historical approaches to the most essential of all social organizations: the family. Some aspects of the history of the family to be covered will include the analysis of the continuous interaction between economic forms of production and the social conditions which accompany them; analysis of the kind of societies which have favoured various family strategies at particular times; the role of the emancipation of women; the changing position of children in the family; the impact of new technologies and the increasing mechanization of work on the family. This theme will be approached through the examination of various debates and methodological approaches related to family history and the analysis of a variety of primary and secondary sources (including cinematographic material, legal documents and personal narrative accounts).

  • Art and Ideas: 1850-1950
  • 00003
  • Julie Johnson
  • The hundred-year period between 1850 and 1950 was one of profound dynamism and change and has left a legacy that continues to have an impact on the world we inhabit today. This course explores some of the historical events that shaped this powerful age – including the revolutions of 1848, industrialization, urban growth, empire, and the two world wars – and emphasizes the links between their social and economic elements and their relationship to the art and ideas of the time. Particular focus is given to artistic, literary, and philosophical movements that emphasized change and innovation, and that challenged the existing order. Although the principal focus of the course is on Europe and the United States, non-western views and experiences will also be examined. The competencies include the ability to identify specific situations related to art and ideas from 1850 to 1950, using historical concepts, approaches, and primary and secondary sources.

330-401-DW
Advanced Studies in History
2 sections
  • The Global 1960s: Times of Change
  • 00001
  • Maryann Farkas
  • This course examines the 1960s around the globe through the lenses of change and challenges to the established order. While North Americans might remember the hippy “summer of love” in San Francisco or the civil rights movement in the American south, the 1960s saw change, mutually inspired and supported and often interlinked, around the world. We will look at such diverse movements as the civil rights movement, women’s liberation and the gay rights movement in the West, the 1968 revolution in France, struggles against the Soviet Union in Eastern Europe, decolonization in the developing world, and changes in pop culture. These diverse movements will be examined in various countries to draw out the links and the differences between them around the world.   • Right Wing Politics in the USA: KKK to Trump
  • 00002
  • Pierre L'Heureux
  • This class examines the topic of right-wing politics in the United States, from the 1920s to the presidency of Donald Trump. It explores more specifically the concept of conservatism and the different forms it has taken in the context of the United States through the study of several topics such as the rebirth of the Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s, white supremacy and white nationalism, fascism and antisemitism before WWII, McCarthyism and anti-communism, the 1968 election and the rise of modern conservatism, the southern strategy and the politics of race, anti-feminism, the tax protest movement, the militia movement, Christian nationalism and the Moral Majority, the Tea Party movement and Trumpism. Through the reading of several secondary sources, students will produce a project using historical knowledge, methodology, and concepts learnt both in this class and their 100, 200 or/and 300 level History courses.
